Martin Wiu

I build complex systems, race long distances, and think obsessively about why we do any of it.

Hey! I'm Martin, a 30-year-old Norwegian software engineer. I currently work at KONGSBERG as an individual contributor, creating surveillance systems for maritime security. For my health and, at times, my peace of mind, I train for long-distance triathlon. I also enjoy learning about psychology and philosophy, so much so that I would almost call myself an aspiring philosopher. I am a supporter of the non-profit organization 80,000 Hours, which focuses on how to use your career to tackle the most pressing global challenges humanity faces. I highly recommend you take a look!

My engineering philosophy follows the spirit of Edsger Dijkstra and Leonardo da Vinci: simplicity is the highest form of quality (and obviously, Go is my favorite programming language). Through designing and maintaining multiple long-lived complex systems, I have learned, as many programmers before me have, that simple is, in fact, difficult enough. I used to care deeply about “the craft” and always strived to create the most beautifully composed code. But I have started using my love for learning to become a better engineer, focusing on breadth, leading teams, doing system design, and occasionally diving deep into a topic to seek mastery.

The reason I got into triathlon and training for an Ironman is rather simple. A coworker challenged me to run a half-marathon with very little training. I said yes and shook his hand so quickly that any particle of doubt wouldn’t even have had the time to form. I finished the half-marathon, which led to a marathon, which led to signing up for an Ironman. For every Ironman event I do, I will create a fundraiser for research on pulmonary fibrosis, a cause close to my heart, and donate all the proceeds.

My interest in psychology and philosophy wasn’t accidental. Like many young men, I went through a brief period of struggling with my mental health, and that experience pushed me to understand the mind more deeply. Psychology led me to philosophy, and both played major roles in helping me move forward. It was the hardest period of my life, but I’m genuinely grateful for the direction it set me on.
